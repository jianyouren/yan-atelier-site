## 4. Craft — Technical Specifics (be PRECISE)

### Plique-à-jour (空窗珐琅) — Hu Yan's signature technique
- **What:** Enamel held in openwork wire (no metal backing). When the piece is held to light, the enamel passes light like a stained-glass window.
- **Historical origin:** Earliest celebrated piece is the Mérode Cup (c. 1400), pre-dating Art Nouveau by ~500 years. Art Nouveau (Lalique, Eugène Feuillâtre, André Fernand Thesmar) was a REVIVAL of plique-à-jour, not its invention.
- **Today:** Practiced by relatively few independent workshops worldwide. YÀN is one of the few independent Chinese workshops producing it consistently.
- **Why it's hard:** Without a metal backing, the molten enamel can sag, crack, or fall through the openwork during firing. Multiple firings at controlled temperatures, with very specific pigment chemistry, are required.

### Cloisonné (景泰蓝) — related but distinct
- **What:** Enamel filling cells formed by silver or copper wire, with a metal backing.
- **Different from plique-à-jour:** has a backing; less translucent.
- **Met Museum dates earliest secure Chinese cloisonné to Xuande era (1426-1435).** Common claim that "Jingtai era was the peak" is 17th-century retrospective attribution — should be hedged if discussed.
- YÀN does some cloisonné but is primarily known for plique-à-jour + mineral enamel.

### Mineral Enamel (矿石珐琅) — YÀN's enamel pigment family
- **What:** Pigments derived from natural mineral stones (not synthetic).
- **Why it matters:** Color stability over decades. Some pigments (cobalt-blue, certain reds, deep greens) are extremely rare and as expensive as gold per gram.
- **Result:** Pieces don't yellow or fade. Heritage potential.

### High-fire (高温珐琅) — YÀN's process
- **Temperature: ~800°C.** Always state precisely. Never write "high temperature" or "700-850°C range" — YÀN does **800°C high-fire**.
- **Why:** High-fire gives glass-like clarity and 200+ year durability. Low-fire (~580-650°C) gives softer glaze, less durable.
- Each piece passes through 5-10 kiln firings, depending on color layering.

### Wire-inlay (掐丝 · qiā-sī)
- The technique of bending silver wire and soldering it into cell forms before enamel is laid.
- All YÀN pieces use 掐丝 as the structural base.

### Process steps (in order)
1. **掐丝** — silver wire bent and soldered to design
2. **点蓝** — mineral enamel pigments laid into the cells
3. **入炉** — kiln-fired at ~800°C (multiple times)
4. **打磨** — polishing
5. **检视** — final inspection at the bench

### Materials used
- 925 silver (primary metal)
- Natural mineral enamel pigments
- Occasional inserts: freshwater pearls, garnet, agate, amber, red carnelian, jade
