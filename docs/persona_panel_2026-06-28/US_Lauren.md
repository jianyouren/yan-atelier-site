## First fold (3-second parse)

I land on a beige page. Top: a thin overline "Translucent enamel jewellery · fired in Yunnan." Headline: **"Wear it. People will ask where you found it."** Subhead: "Five to ten firings, every one slightly different — no one else will be wearing the same piece." On the right, a worn butterfly-wing pendant at the collarbone with a pearl drop. The thin promo bar at the very top says **"From $369 · 7 in stock this batch · 14-day return."**

My honest 3-second read: **the headline is good — it's the only headline on a Yunnan enamel site that talks like an SSENSE caption instead of like a museum placard.** "People will ask where you found it" is exactly the JTBD ("花小钱装大逼" minus the Mandarin) and I clocked it immediately. The subhead is also strong — "no one else will be wearing the same piece" is the same insider-luxury hook Wing Yau / Polly Wales lean on. The price floor in the promo bar ($369) tells me this isn't trying to be Anna Hu, which is the right call.

What pulls me up short: the hero image **reads as AI-rendered or heavily retouched** — the butterfly-wing-on-skin shot is too lit, the skin is too smooth, the pendant looks like a 3D pose. I don't see a model breathing. I would not bounce immediately, but my "is this real?" alarm is now armed for the rest of the page.

I scroll. The dual-door band hits me with a giant **"AI assisted render"** badge on the left door. I appreciate the disclosure honesty — but the fact that the image on the first product door is AI is **exactly the tell that makes me close a tab on Etsy.** I have not seen a single Catbird / WWAKE / Aurate page admit an image is AI.

## What works for the US market

- **The hero H1.** "People will ask where you found it" — this is the only sentence on the whole page written from my side of the table, in Cut/SSENSE register. Keep it.
- **The Craft page essay.** Genuinely substantiated. Citations to Met, V&A, Walters, Britannica. Distinguishes plique-à-jour from cloisonné without exoticizing either. This is the page I'd send to my mother before buying.
- **The Renaissance framing in the Plique section.** "Renaissance goldsmiths did" — by anchoring the craft to a Western lineage instead of "ancient Chinese tradition," you've sidestepped the exoticism trap. This is sharper than Mejuri's "modern fine jewelry" word-salad.
- **DDP / "duties included" copy in the Terms.** Crucial for US buyers from a CN site. The fact that it's stated plainly (not buried) is the right move.
- **The promo bar specificity.** "7 in stock this batch" with a number (not "limited edition") feels real, not synthetic.

## What feels off / wrong-register for the US market

- **The Maker section voice.** "My mother, Hu Yan, took up silver wire and enamel when she was very young, and has not put them down since. She had no teacher." This is first-person-son writing about his mother. WWAKE / Catbird / Aurate would never. The brand voice on indie US fine sites is third-person editorial ("Hu Yan, founder, working in Yunnan…"). The current voice reads either as (a) a Substack essay or (b) translated-from-Chinese, both of which break the editorial-luxury spell. Lauren-class readers register "narrator = founder's son" and downgrade trust.
- **The Reserve flow is a mailto.** This is the single biggest credibility drop. Indie sites my friends shop on — Catbird, Aurate, Stone & Strand, Susan Alexandra — all have one-click Shop Pay. The 4-step "we'll email you a payment link within 24 hours" reads as a 阿里巴巴 Trade Assurance flow, not a fine jewelry checkout. I would not complete it for $369, much less $729.
- **"AI assisted render" badge on the dual-door.** Honest, but it tells me that the brand cannot afford a Catbird-grade product photographer. Lauren-class buyers expect real photos of real jewelry. A worn shot that is openly AI is a contradiction in terms for indie fine.
- **Hero image is uncannily lit.** Even without the badge on the dual-door, the hero butterfly-wing pendant has a too-clean, too-symmetrical quality. The pearl drop hangs in a way that reads as 3D render rather than photographed. WWAKE's hero images all have grain, shadow, a real model with a real neck.
- **Press quotes (in the data, even if currently hidden display:none).** "Hainan Daily · 2023" and "Sanya Cultural Fair · 2023" — these are not press I recognize, and if surfaced they would *hurt* not help. US indie sites quote The Cut, Vogue Business, T Magazine. Better to show no press than regional CN press.
- **"Heritage re-registration" in the Promise list.** I don't know what this is and the word "heritage" applied to a 2026 piece reads as overclaim. Catbird's analog is "lifetime free polish" — concrete, not metaphorical.

## The ONE deal-breaker

**The Reserve flow is a mailto pretending to be a checkout.** I clicked "Get this piece" expecting Stripe; got a 4-step contact form that ends with "We've prepared this reservation request in your mail client. Please press send." For a $369–$729 indie purchase from a brand I've never heard of, in a country I don't have a direct payment rail to, **the absence of a real card-on-file checkout is the single thing that sends me back to Instagram to check whether anyone I know has actually received a piece.** And when I find no UGC and no real press, I bounce.

The hero copy is excellent. The craft essay is excellent. The plique framing is excellent. **None of it survives the moment the buy button reveals itself to be an email draft.** Fix this one thing and the score jumps from 5.8 to ~7.5 overnight.

## What would close the sale

Two things, in order: **(1) Stripe / Shop Pay live with a Buy Now button that takes my card on the page itself — not a mailto, not a "reservation request," an actual checkout** (the SHOPIFY_VARIANTS scaffold in the code suggests this is on the roadmap; ship it first, not last). **(2) Replace the hero image and the dual-door left image with real photographs of the actual pieces being worn by a real person** — even an iPhone shot at the bench would convert better than the current AI-assisted renders, because Lauren-class buyers read "uncanny lighting" as "this brand cannot show me what I'm actually buying."
